Stationery & Uniforms

New term, new uniform

Each January and September, Msingi fits children heading back to school with a full uniform, shoes, and a term's worth of exercise books — often the difference between attending and staying home.

"My daughter stopped hiding her old shoes under the desk." — Parent, Ongata Rongai
Widows' Nutrition

A monthly basket, not a one-off

540 widows across Kajiado receive a monthly food basket of maize, beans, cooking oil, and vegetables, distributed through a rotating network of community elders who know each household by name.

"I no longer choose between medicine and dinner." — Programme recipient, age 68
Boychild Recovery

Counselling, then a trade

The Boychild programme pairs young men recovering from drug dependency with a counsellor and, once stabilised, a mentor in a trade — welding, boda repair, or carpentry — so recovery leads somewhere.

"They didn't just get me clean, they got me a chair at the workshop." — Programme graduate, 22

Programs

Three ways we show up, on repeat.

Msingi runs three standing programs rather than one-off campaigns. Each has its own budget, its own field officer, and its own line in the ledger — so you always know which door your gift walked through.

Stationery & Uniforms

School Essentials Fund

Every January and September, field officers visit partner primary schools to identify children turned away or shamed for missing uniforms or books. Each child receives a full uniform, shoes, and a term's stationery — sized and sourced from local tailors and stationers rather than shipped in bulk.

Nutrition

Widows' Nutrition Program

540 widows across Kajiado County receive a monthly food basket — maize, beans, cooking oil, and seasonal vegetables — delivered through a rotating network of community elders who visit each household in person. Elders also flag health concerns back to our team.

Boychild

Boychild Recovery & Mentorship

A two-stage program for young men working to overcome drug dependency: first, weekly counselling with a licensed addiction counsellor; then, once stabilised, placement with a mentor in a trade — welding, boda repair, or carpentry — so recovery has somewhere to go. Families are involved throughout.

About

Why Msingi exists.

Msingi started in 2021 when three teachers in Kajiado County pooled their own salaries to buy uniforms for children being sent home for lacking them. What began as an informal fund is now a registered trust — still run by people who grew up in the communities it serves.

What we believe

Three commitments that shape every program decision.

01

Transparency by default

Every gift is logged the day it lands. Our public ledger and annual audits exist so no one has to take our word for it.

02

Dignity over spectacle

We don't photograph people at their lowest moment for a campaign. Stories are shared with consent, and often in the recipient's own words.

03

Local-first delivery

Field officers, elders, and mentors are drawn from the communities we work in — they're neighbours first, staff second.

How we got here

Five years, three programs.

2021

An informal fund among teachers

Three primary school teachers in Kajiado begin quietly covering uniform costs for pupils being turned away.

2022

Registered as a trust; Widows' Nutrition launches

Msingi Community Trust is formally registered. The first monthly food baskets reach 60 widows in Kajiado Central.

2023

School Essentials Fund formalised

The uniform fund becomes a standing program with its own budget line, reaching over 1,200 children in its first year.

2024

Boychild Recovery & Mentorship begins

In response to requests from mothers in the Widows' Nutrition program, Msingi launches counselling and mentorship support for young men.

2026

USD 4M disbursed, three programs strong

Msingi now supports 3,180 children, 540 widows, and 30 young men in recovery — with M-Pesa and PayPal giving open to anyone, anywhere.
